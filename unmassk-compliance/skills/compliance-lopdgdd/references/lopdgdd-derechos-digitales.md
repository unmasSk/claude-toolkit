# LOPDGDD — Digital Rights (Title X)

Reference for the digital rights established in Title X of the LOPDGDD (Articles 79–97). These rights go beyond the GDPR and are specific to Spain. They are particularly relevant for digital services, social networks, employment contexts, and public administration.

Source: Ley Orgánica 3/2018, Articles 79–97. Full text: https://www.boe.es/buscar/act.php?id=BOE-A-2018-16673

---

## Overview

Title X LOPDGDD (Articles 79–97) establishes a catalog of digital rights that complement the GDPR's data subject rights. These rights apply in employment, educational, and digital service contexts and create obligations for employers, service providers, and public administrations beyond standard GDPR requirements.

---

## Right to Internet Access (Article 81)

Citizens have the right of access to the internet as a fundamental element for the full development of civic, social, educational, and economic life.

**Practical impact:** Services provided by public administrations must be accessible online. Digital exclusion must be considered in service design.

---

## Right to Digital Security (Article 82)

Citizens have the right to digital security, including the protection of their information systems, data, and electronic communications from interference and attacks.

**Practical impact:** Organizations handling citizens' data must implement proportionate security measures. Relates to the security obligations under Article 32 GDPR.

---

## Right to Digital Education (Article 83)

Citizens have the right to receive basic digital literacy training. The public education system must incorporate digital competence at all levels.

**Practical impact:** Educational institutions processing student data must include digital rights awareness in curricula.

---

## Right to Online Privacy (Articles 84–86)

### Right to Privacy on the Internet (Article 84)

Data subjects have the right to protection of their privacy on digital services, including protection of their reputation, image, and identity.

### Right to Privacy in the Use of Digital Devices in the Workplace (Article 87)

Employees have the right to privacy in the use of devices provided by the employer. Employers may establish usage policies but must inform employees of the scope and purpose of any monitoring before implementing it.

**Obligations for employers:**
- Draft and communicate a clear acceptable use policy for work devices
- Specify permitted and prohibited uses
- Inform employees of any monitoring measures before they are activated
- Monitoring must be proportionate and limited to what is necessary

### Right to Digital Disconnection in the Workplace (Article 88)

Employees have the right to digital disconnection outside working hours to protect their rest time, leave, and personal and family privacy.

**Obligations for employers:**
- Establish an internal digital disconnection policy
- Consult with employees' representatives when drafting the policy
- The policy must define the modalities for exercising digital disconnection
- Applies to telework and distance work situations

---

## Right to Privacy in Video Surveillance at Work (Article 89)

Employees and public employees have the right to privacy against the use of geolocation and audio-visual surveillance systems in the workplace.

**Rules for workplace video surveillance:**
- Employer may install cameras in the workplace for security and production monitoring purposes
- Data subjects (employees) must be informed in advance (signage is sufficient for security purposes — the "hollowed-out clause" applies)
- Cameras may not be placed in rest areas, changing rooms, or toilets
- Captured images must not be kept longer than one month except when related to a proven infringement or legal proceedings
- Covert monitoring is only permitted in cases of well-founded suspicion of serious unlawful conduct by an employee; it must be proportionate and temporary

**Geolocation systems:**
- Same prior information obligation applies
- Legitimate interest or contractual necessity is typically the legal basis
- Data subjects must be informed of their right to object

---

## Right to Privacy in Work Relationships (Article 90)

Workers have the right to privacy regarding their personal data shared with employers. Processing of such data must be limited to what is necessary for the employment relationship.

---

## Right to Be Forgotten in Internet Searches (Article 93)

Data subjects have the right to request that search engines remove from search results links containing information about them that is inadequate, inaccurate, not relevant, not updated, or excessive.

**Key points:**
- Applies to general search engines operating in Spain
- The right must be balanced against public interest in the information
- Journalists, public figures, and persons of public interest have reduced right to be forgotten
- Requests must be submitted directly to the search engine operator

---

## Right to Be Forgotten on Social Networks (Article 93, paragraph 2)

Data subjects have the right to request that social network operators and equivalent services remove personal data that they provided as minors, or that was provided without meeting the requirements for valid consent.

**Obligations for social network operators:**
- Must process erasure requests within one month
- Must provide a straightforward mechanism to exercise the right
- Must not retain the data after erasure unless a legal basis continues to apply

---

## Right to Portability on Social Networks (Article 94)

Data subjects have the right to receive personal data they have provided to social networks and equivalent services in a structured, commonly used, machine-readable format, and to transmit that data to another service provider.

**Obligations:**
- Data must be provided without undue delay and free of charge
- Format must allow interoperability between services
- Applies to data actively provided by the user (content, connections, preferences)

---

## Right to Digital Will / Testament (Article 96)

After the death of an individual, their heirs, relatives, or persons designated by the deceased may request access to or deletion of the deceased's digital content from service providers.

**Practical rules:**
- The deceased may have left instructions about the treatment of their digital data after death (digital will)
- In the absence of instructions, close relatives may request access or erasure
- Minors' data: parents may always request erasure of data processed during minority
- Service providers must respond within one month of receiving the request with valid accreditation of the relationship

## Summary Table for Digital Projects

| Right | Article | Who Is Affected | Key Obligation |
|-------|---------|-----------------|----------------|
| Digital disconnection | Art. 88 | Employers | Draft and publish internal policy |
| Privacy in work device use | Art. 87 | Employers | Prior information obligation; proportionate monitoring only |
