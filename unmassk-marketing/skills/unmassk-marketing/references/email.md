# Email Reference

Complete reference for automated email sequences, lifecycle email programs, cold email writing, and email platform tooling.

---

## Automated Email Sequences

### Core Principles

1. **One email, one job** -- each email has one primary purpose and one main CTA. Do not try to do everything.
2. **Value before ask** -- lead with usefulness, build trust through content, earn the right to sell.
3. **Relevance over volume** -- fewer, better emails win. Segment for relevance. Quality beats frequency.
4. **Clear path forward** -- every email moves them somewhere. Links should do something useful. Make next steps obvious.

### Sequence Length Guidelines

| Sequence Type | Length | Timing |
|---------------|--------|--------|
| Welcome | 5-7 emails over 12-14 days | 1-2 days apart early, then wider |
| Lead nurture | 6-8 emails over 2-3 weeks | 2-4 days apart |
| Onboarding | 5-7 emails over 14 days | 1-3 days apart |
| Re-engagement | 3-4 emails over 2 weeks | 2-4 days apart |

Depends on sales cycle length, product complexity, and relationship stage. B2B: avoid weekends. B2C: test weekends. Send at local time.

## Welcome Sequence (Post-Signup)

**Trigger:** User signs up
**Goal:** Activate, build trust, convert
**Length:** 5-7 emails over 12-14 days

| Email | Day | Purpose | Content |
|-------|-----|---------|---------|
| 1 | Immediate | Welcome + deliver value | Deliver what was promised (lead magnet, access). Single next action. Set expectations for future emails. |
| 2 | Day 1-2 | Quick win | Enable small success. Build confidence. Link to helpful resource. |
| 3 | Day 3-4 | Story/why | Origin story or mission. Connect emotionally. Show you understand their problem. |
| 4 | Day 5-6 | Social proof | Case study or testimonial. Relatable to their situation. Soft CTA to explore. |
| 5 | Day 7-8 | Overcome objection | Address common hesitation. Reframe the obstacle. Show easy path forward. |
| 6 | Day 9-11 | Core feature | Highlight underused capability. Show clear benefit. Direct CTA to try it. |
| 7 | Day 12-14 | Conversion | Summarize value. Clear offer. Urgency if appropriate. Risk reversal (guarantee, trial). |

## Lead Nurture Sequence (Pre-Sale)

**Trigger:** Lead magnet download or content engagement
**Goal:** Build trust, demonstrate expertise, convert
**Length:** 6-8 emails over 2-3 weeks

| Email | Day | Purpose | Content |
|-------|-----|---------|---------|
| 1 | Immediate | Deliver + introduce | Deliver the lead magnet. Brief intro to who you are. Preview what is coming. |
| 2 | Day 2-3 | Expand on topic | Related insight to lead magnet. Establish expertise. Light CTA to content. |
| 3 | Day 4-5 | Problem deep-dive | Articulate their problem deeply. Show you understand. Hint at solution. |
| 4 | Day 6-8 | Solution framework | Your approach/methodology. Educational, not salesy. Builds toward your product. |
| 5 | Day 9-11 | Case study | Real results from real customer. Specific and relatable. Soft CTA. |
| 6 | Day 12-14 | Differentiation | Why your approach is different. Address alternatives. Build preference. |
| 7 | Day 15-18 | Objection handler | Common concern addressed. FAQ or myth-busting. Reduce friction. |
| 8 | Day 19-21 | Direct offer | Clear pitch. Strong value proposition. Specific CTA. Urgency if available. |

## Onboarding Sequence (Product Users)

**Trigger:** User signs up for product
**Goal:** Activate, drive to aha moment, upgrade
**Length:** 5-7 emails over 14 days
**Coordination:** Email supports in-app onboarding, does not duplicate it.

| Email | Day | Purpose | Content |
|-------|-----|---------|---------|
| 1 | Immediate | Welcome + first step | Confirm signup. One critical action. Link directly to that action. |
| 2 | Day 1 | Getting started help | If they have not completed step 1. Quick tip or video. Support option. |
| 3 | Day 2-3 | Feature highlight | Key feature they should know. Specific use case. In-app link. |
| 4 | Day 4-5 | Success story | Customer who succeeded. Relatable journey. Motivational. |
| 5 | Day 7 | Check-in | How is it going? Ask for feedback. Offer help. |
| 6 | Day 10-12 | Advanced tip | Power feature for engaged users. Level-up content. |
| 7 | Day 14+ | Upgrade/expand | For trial: conversion push. For free: upgrade prompt. For paid: expansion. |

## Re-Engagement Sequence

**Trigger:** 30-60 days of inactivity
**Goal:** Win back or clean list
**Length:** 3-4 emails over 2 weeks

| Email | Timing | Purpose | Content |
|-------|--------|---------|---------|
| 1 | Day 0 | Check-in | Genuine concern tone. Ask what happened. Easy win to re-engage. |
| 2 | Day 2-3 | Value reminder | Remind of past value. What is new since they left. Quick CTA. |
| 3 | Day 5-7 | Incentive | Offer if appropriate. Limited time. Clear CTA. |
| 4 | Day 10-14 | Last chance | Honest and direct. One-click to stay or go. Clean the list if no response. |

## Email Types by Category

### Onboarding Emails

- **New users series** (5-7 emails, 14 days) -- drive to aha moment
- **New customers series** (3-5 emails, 14 days) -- reinforce purchase decision, different from new user series
- **Key onboarding step reminders** -- triggered when user has not completed critical setup step after X time
- **New user invites** -- when existing user invites teammate, 3 email mini-sequence

### Retention Emails

- **Upgrade to paid** -- triggered by engagement, trial ending, or usage limits (3-5 emails)
- **Upgrade to higher plan** -- triggered at 80-90% of plan limits or power user behavior
- **Ask for review** -- after positive support interaction, milestone, or renewal (NOT after billing issues)
- **Proactive support** -- triggered by usage drops, failed actions, or repeated help doc views
- **Product usage reports** -- weekly/monthly value demonstration with key metrics
- **NPS survey** -- quarterly or post-milestone, follow up based on score (promoters: ask for review, detractors: personal outreach)
- **Referral program** -- after positive NPS, customer achievement, or renewal

### Billing Emails

- **Switch to annual** -- calculate exact savings, lock in current price messaging, best timing around monthly renewal
- **Failed payment recovery** -- 4 email sequence over 7-14 days escalating from friendly to final warning
- **Cancellation survey** -- immediate, learn why with opportunity to save
- **Upcoming renewal reminder** -- 14 or 30 days before renewal with amount and date

### Usage Emails

- **Daily/weekly/monthly summaries** -- key metrics at a glance, achievements, suggestions
- **Key event notifications** -- first action completed, Nth thing created, goal achieved, usage streak
- **Milestone celebrations** -- celebration tone, specific achievement, comparison context, next milestone

### Win-Back Emails

- **Expired trials** (3-4 emails over 30 days):
  - Day 1: Trial ended, here is what you are missing
  - Day 7: What held you back? (gather feedback)
  - Day 14: Incentive offer (discount, extended trial)
  - Day 30: Final reach-out, door is open

- **Cancelled customers** (2-3 emails over 90 days):
  - Day 30: What is new since you left
  - Day 60: We have addressed [common reason]
  - Day 90: Special offer to return

Segment by engagement level: high engagement (remove friction), low engagement (fresh start, more onboarding), no engagement (ask what happened, offer demo).

### Campaign Emails

- **Monthly roundup / newsletter** -- product updates, customer stories, educational content
- **Seasonal promotions** -- Black Friday, New Year, industry-specific (2-4 email burst: announcement, reminder, last chance)
- **Product updates** -- what is new, why it matters, how to use it, who asked for it
- **Industry news roundup** -- curated news with commentary
- **Pricing updates** -- announce 30-60 days before, transparent about why, grandfather if possible

## Email Copy Guidelines

### Structure

Every email follows this pattern:
1. **Hook** -- first line grabs attention
2. **Context** -- why this matters to them
3. **Value** -- the useful content
4. **CTA** -- what to do next
5. **Sign-off** -- human, warm close

### Formatting

- Short paragraphs (1-3 sentences)
- White space between sections
- Bullet points for scanability
- Bold for emphasis (sparingly)
- Mobile-first (most read on phone)

### Tone

- Conversational, not formal
- First-person (I/we) and second-person (you)
- Active voice
- Match your brand but lean friendly
- Read it out loud -- does it sound human?

### Length

| Type | Word Count |
|------|-----------|
| Transactional | 50-125 words |
| Educational | 150-300 words |
| Story-driven | 300-500 words |

### CTA Guidelines

- Buttons for primary actions, links for secondary actions
- One clear primary CTA per email
- Button text: action + outcome (e.g., "Start Saving Time" not "Click Here")

### Personalization

**Merge fields:** First name (fallback to "there" or "friend"), company name (B2B), relevant data (usage, plan).

**Dynamic content:** Based on segment, behavior, and stage.

**Triggered emails:** Action-based sends are more relevant than time-based. Examples: feature used, milestone hit, inactivity detected.

### Segmentation Strategies

**By behavior:** Openers vs non-openers, clickers vs non-clickers, active vs inactive.

**By stage:** Trial vs paid, new vs long-term, engaged vs at-risk.

**By profile:** Industry/role (B2B), use case/goal, company size.

### Testing and Optimization

Test in order of impact:
1. Subject lines (highest impact)
2. Send times
3. Email length
4. CTA placement and copy
5. Personalization level
6. Sequence timing

A/B test one variable at a time with sufficient sample size and statistical significance.

### Email Benchmarks

| Metric | Benchmark |
|--------|-----------|
| Open rate | 20-40% |
| Click rate | 2-5% |
| Unsubscribe rate | Keep under 0.5% |
| Conversion rate | Specific to sequence goal |

## Subject Line Strategy

### Automated Sequences

- Clear over clever
- Specific over vague
- Benefit or curiosity-driven
- 40-60 characters ideal
- Test emoji (they are polarizing)

**Patterns that work:**
- Question: "Still struggling with X?"
- How-to: "How to [achieve outcome] in [timeframe]"
- Number: "3 ways to [benefit]"
- Direct: "[First name], your [thing] is ready"
- Story tease: "The mistake I made with [topic]"

### Preview Text

- Extends the subject line
- 90-140 characters
- Do not repeat subject line
- Complete the thought or add intrigue

## Cold Email Writing

### Writing Principles

**Write like a peer, not a vendor.** The email should read like it came from someone who understands their world. Use contractions. Read it aloud. If it sounds like marketing copy, rewrite it.

**Every sentence must earn its place.** Cold email is ruthlessly short. If a sentence does not move the reader toward replying, cut it. The best cold emails feel like they could have been shorter.

**Personalization must connect to the problem.** If you remove the personalized opening and the email still makes sense, the personalization is not working. The observation should naturally lead into why you are reaching out.

**Lead with their world, not yours.** "You/your" should dominate over "I/we." Do not open with who you are or what your company does.

**One ask, low friction.** Interest-based CTAs ("Worth exploring?" / "Would this be useful?") beat meeting requests. One CTA per email.

### Voice Calibration

- **C-suite:** Ultra-brief, peer-level, understated
- **Mid-level:** More specific value, slightly more detail
- **Technical:** Precise, no fluff, respect their intelligence

### Cold Email Frameworks

**PAS -- Problem, Agitate, Solution (default):** Identify pain -> amplify consequences -> present solution + soft CTA. Best for problem-aware but not solution-aware prospects.

**BAB -- Before, After, Bridge:** Current painful situation -> ideal future -> your product as the bridge. Best for transformation-driven offers.

**QVC -- Question, Value, CTA:** Targeted pain question -> brief value -> direct next step. Best for C-suite brevity.

**AIDA -- Attention, Interest, Desire, Action:** Hook/stat -> address challenge -> social proof/outcome -> clear CTA. Best for data-driven prospects.

**PPP -- Praise, Picture, Push:** Genuine compliment -> how things could be better -> gentle push. Best for senior prospects.

**Star-Story-Solution:** Introduce character (customer) -> tell challenge narrative -> reveal results. Best for strong success stories.

**SCQ -- Situation, Complication, Question:** Current reality -> complicating challenge -> question that speaks to need. Best for consultative selling.

**ACCA -- Awareness, Comprehension, Conviction, Action:** Contrarian hook -> explain benefit -> provide proof -> strong CTA. Best for analytical buyers.

**3C's (Alex Berman):** Compliment -> Case Study -> CTA. Best for agency/services outreach.

**Mouse Trap (Will Allred):** Observation + binary value-prop question. 1-2 sentences total. Maximum brevity.

**Justin Michael Method:** Trigger/Pain -> solution hint -> binary CTA. 1-3 sentences. Spend max 1 minute on personalization.

**Observation-Problem-Proof-Ask:** Noticed X -> which usually means Y challenge -> we helped Z with that -> interested?

**Vanilla Ice Cream (Lavender/Will Allred):** Observation -> Problem/Insight -> Credibility -> Solution -> Call-to-Conversation. Universal "base" framework that works everywhere. Five parts.

**PASTOR (Ray Edwards):** Problem -> Amplify -> Story -> Testimony -> Offer -> Response. Best for longer-form or multi-email sequences -- consulting, education, complex B2B services. Each element can be developed across separate touches.

### Personalization System (4 Levels)

**Level 1 -- Basic (merge tags):** First name, company name, job title. Table stakes. Approximately 5% lift.

**Level 2 -- Industry/segment:** Industry-specific pain points, trends, regulatory challenges. Scalable via micro-segmentation.

**Level 3 -- Role-level:** Challenges specific to their role and seniority. E.g., "As Head of Sales, keeping pipeline steady is probably your biggest headache."

**Level 4 -- Individual (gold standard):** Specific, timely observations about that person connected to the problem you solve. E.g., "Noticed you are hiring 3 SDRs -- sounds like you are scaling outbound fast."

### The 3-Minute Personalization System

1. Build a research stack of top 10 buying signals (5 company triggers, 5 person triggers)
2. Build a 3x3 template: personalization attached to a problem, problem you solve, one-sentence solution + low-friction CTA
3. Create 5 trigger templates -- pre-written personalization paragraphs for each trigger with smooth segue into the problem

### Subject Lines for Cold Email

Short, boring, internal-looking. The subject line's only job is to get the email opened.

- 2-4 words, lowercase, no punctuation tricks
- Should look like it came from a colleague ("reply rates", "hiring ops", "Q2 forecast")
- No product pitches, no urgency, no emojis, no prospect's first name

**Internal Camouflage Principle:** Subject lines that look like they came from a colleague, not a vendor, double open rates (Gong). Buyers mentally categorize before opening -- if it looks like sales, it is filtered. High-performing examples: "reply rates" · "trial delays" · "hiring ops" · "employee turnover" · "Q2 forecast" · "new patients" · "personalization issue" · "second page"

**Data points:**
- 2-word subject lines get 60% more opens than 5-word
- Lowercase has highest open rates (85M+ emails analyzed)
- First name in subject line results in 12% fewer replies (signals automation)
- Contextual personalization works: pain points, competitors, trigger events
- Salesy language reduces opens by 17.9%
- Numbers and percentages reduce opens by 46%

**C-suite subject lines:** Ultra-concise, human, understated. Examples: "{{companyInitiative}}", "thank you", "an update". Executives decide in seconds.

### Follow-Up Sequences

55% of replies come from follow-ups, not the initial email. 48% of salespeople never follow up even once.

**Optimal cadence (3-5 total emails, increasing gaps):**

| Touch | Day | Notes |
|-------|-----|-------|
| Initial email | 0 | Maximum personalization investment |
| Follow-up 1 | 3 | Waiting 3 days increases response by up to 31% |
| Follow-up 2 | 7-8 | Different angle |
| Follow-up 3 | 14 | New value piece |
| Follow-up 4 | 21-28 | Breakup email |

**Best days:** Tuesday through Thursday (Thursday peaks at 6.87% reply rate).
**Best times:** 9-11 AM or 1-3 PM in prospect's local time.
**Avoid:** Monday mornings, Friday afternoons.

**Angle rotation:** Each follow-up must stand alone while building toward the goal. Add one new value proposition per email.

| Email | Angle | Purpose |
|-------|-------|---------|
| Initial | Personalized hook + core value prop + soft CTA | Introduce problem/solution |
| Follow-up 1 | Different angle, new value piece | Show additional benefit |
| Follow-up 2 | Social proof / case study | Build credibility |
| Follow-up 3 | New insight, industry trend, or resource | Demonstrate expertise |
| Follow-up 4 | Breakup -- acknowledge silence, leave door open | Trigger loss aversion |

### The Breakup Email

Leverages loss aversion. 10-15% response rates from breakup emails with cold prospects.

Structure: Acknowledge you have reached out multiple times -> validate their potential lack of interest -> state this is your final email -> leave the door open.

**1-2-3 Format (reduces friction to near zero):**
```
Since I have not heard back, I will keep it simple. Reply with a number:
1 -- Interested, let's talk
2 -- Not now, check back in 3 months
3 -- Not interested, please stop
```

If you send a breakup email, honor it. Do not contact the prospect again.

### Phrases That Kill Response Rates

- "I never heard back" -- 12% drop in meeting booking rate
- "Just checking in" -- zero value, signals laziness
- "Bumping this to the top of your inbox" -- presumptuous
- "Did you see my last email?" -- guilt-tripping
- "Following up on my previous message" -- generic, adds nothing

### What to Avoid

- Opening with "I hope this email finds you well" or "My name is X and I work at Y"
- Jargon: "synergy," "leverage," "circle back," "best-in-class," "leading provider"
- Feature dumps -- one proof point beats ten features
- HTML, images, or multiple links
- Fake "Re:" or "Fwd:" subject lines
- Identical templates with only {{FirstName}} swapped
- Asking for 30-minute calls in first touch

### Top 15 Mistakes (Ranked by Impact)

1. **Too long** -- 70% of emails above 10th-grade level. Under 75 words = 83% more replies
2. **Too self-focused** -- "We are a leading..." signals sales pitch. Count I/We sentences
3. **No clear value prop** -- 71% of decision-makers ignore irrelevant emails
4. **Generic templates** -- {{FirstName}} is not personalization. Recipients detect instantly
5. **Feature dumping** -- "Great reps lead with problems" (Lavender). One proof point beats ten features
6. **False personalization** -- "Loved your post!" without specifics is transparent
7. **Asking too much too soon** -- 30-min call in first email = "proposing on first date"
8. **Pushy language** -- "Act Now" stacking increases spam flagging by 67%
9. **No CTA** -- Without a clear next step, momentum dies
10. **"Just checking in" follow-ups** -- "I never heard back" = 12% drop in bookings
11. **Wrong tone for audience** -- Founder is not RevOps lead is not sales leader
12. **Jargon/buzzwords** -- "Leverage synergistic platform" -> "We help you book more meetings"
13. **Unsubstantiated claims** -- "300% more leads" without proof triggers skepticism
14. **Too many contacts per company** -- 1-2 people = 7.8% reply; 10+ = 3.8%
15. **Fake urgency** -- Fake "Re:" / "Fwd:" / countdown timers destroy trust

## CLI Scripts for Email Platforms

All CLI tools are zero-dependency Node.js scripts (Node 18+). Set environment variables for auth and run directly.

### mailchimp.js

SMB email marketing, list management, and campaigns.

```bash
# Environment
export MAILCHIMP_API_KEY=xxx-us1

# List audiences
node ${CLAUDE_PLUGIN_ROOT}/skills/unmassk-marketing/scripts/mailchimp.js lists list

# Get audience details
node ${CLAUDE_PLUGIN_ROOT}/skills/unmassk-marketing/scripts/mailchimp.js lists get list_abc123

# List members of an audience
node ${CLAUDE_PLUGIN_ROOT}/skills/unmassk-marketing/scripts/mailchimp.js members list --list-id list_abc123 --count 50

# Add member
node ${CLAUDE_PLUGIN_ROOT}/skills/unmassk-marketing/scripts/mailchimp.js members add --list-id list_abc123 --email user@example.com --status subscribed

# List campaigns
node ${CLAUDE_PLUGIN_ROOT}/skills/unmassk-marketing/scripts/mailchimp.js campaigns list

# Get campaign details
node ${CLAUDE_PLUGIN_ROOT}/skills/unmassk-marketing/scripts/mailchimp.js campaigns get camp_abc123
```

## Composio MCP for Email Platforms

Composio adds MCP access to email and messaging platforms that lack native MCP servers.

**Setup:**
```bash
npx @composio/mcp@latest setup
```

**Available email toolkits via Composio:**

| Tool | Composio Toolkit | Auth | Use For |
|------|-----------------|------|---------|
| ActiveCampaign | `ACTIVECAMPAIGN` | API Key | Contacts, lists, automations |
| Klaviyo | `KLAVIYO` | API Key | Profiles, lists, campaigns |
| Gmail | `GMAIL` | OAuth 2.0 | Read, send, labels, search |
| HubSpot | `HUBSPOT` | OAuth 2.0 | Email sequences, contacts |

**Tools with native MCP (prefer these):**

| Tool | Access | Notes |
|------|--------|-------|
| Mailchimp | Native MCP | Deepest coverage for email marketing |
| Resend | Native MCP | Developer-friendly transactional |

**Example workflows:**

Sync email segments with CRM:
```
"Pull all ActiveCampaign contacts in the 'trial-expiring' list and add them to a HubSpot sequence"
```

Send personalized outreach via Gmail:
```
"Draft and send a personalized email via Gmail to the top 10 leads from our nurture list"
```

Composio handles OAuth token management and refresh. Authenticate each tool via Connect Link on first use.
